My apartment has no heat or hot water.

A clear, step-by-step plan for getting your landlord to fix the problem — written for the moment you're standing in your kitchen wondering what to do.

9 min read Last verified April 13, 2026 Print this page

This is a stressful situation and you shouldn't have to deal with it. The good news: NYC has some of the strongest heat and hot water protections in the country, and the steps below have worked for thousands of tenants.

Read this whole page first if you can. You don't need to take every step — you just need to know what the full path looks like so you can decide how far to go. Most tenants only need the first two or three steps.

If anyone in your apartment is elderly, disabled, a young child, or has a medical condition affected by cold: treat this as urgent. Skip ahead to Step 3 immediately and mention the vulnerable resident when you file your complaint.

Know your rights

What NYC law requires

Your landlord must provide:

  • Hot water year-round, at a minimum of 120°F, every day of the year.
  • Heat during "heat season" (October 1 – May 31)
  • Daytime (6 AM – 10 PM): If it's below 55°F outside, your apartment must be at least 68°F inside.
  • Nighttime (10 PM – 6 AM): Your apartment must be at least 62°F inside, regardless of outside temperature.

Source: NYC Department of Housing Preservation & Development

Before you do anything else

Look up your building's heat complaint history on HPDOnline.

HPD maintains a public database of every housing code violation ever issued in NYC, including heat and hot water. You can look up your building right now and see whether it has a history of heat complaints, whether there are any current open violations, and how your landlord has responded in past winters.

Spend two minutes searching HPDOnline on the site or in the lookup box just below. Look at the relevant violations for heat or hot water. This tells you:

  • Whether your building has been cited for heat violations before — and how many times
  • Whether there are any currently open heat or hot water violations on the building.
  • Whether your landlord typically corrects violations within the legal time frame or lets them languish.

This is leverage. A landlord with a history of heat violations knows exactly what they're doing when the boiler "mysteriously" stops working every January. Walking in with that knowledge changes the conversation entirely.

What to do, step by step

Step 1 5 min, then ongoing

Document the problem right now.

Everything you do after this depends on having evidence. Housing Court and HPD inspections both need documentation. A verbal complaint to your landlord will not hold up if this ends up in court.

How to do it
  1. Get a thermometer. A cheap one from a dollar store or hardware store is fine.
  2. Take temperature readings inside your apartment, and outside if you can (your phone's weather app works). Write down the date, time, indoor temp, and outdoor temp.
  3. Take readings a few times a day, especially during legally-required heat hours.
  4. Photograph your thermometer next to something showing the time — a phone clock, wall clock, or newspaper.
  5. If hot water is the issue, time how long the water runs cold and note the warmest temperature it reaches.
What to save

A running log with every reading. Met Council on Housing has a free Tenant Heat Log PDF designed to be accepted as evidence in Housing Court.

Step 2 10 min

Tell your landlord, in writing.

Most heat and hot water problems are fixed at this step. Many landlords respond once they know there's a problem. Just as important: creating a written record protects you if things escalate. A phone call or text to your super doesn't count as the same kind of evidence as a dated written notice.

How to do it
  1. Write a short, factual message to your landlord or management company. Email is fine. If you don't have an email, send a letter by USPS Certified Mail with Return Receipt — a few dollars, but proof they received it.
  2. Fill in your details below, then copy the message and send it.
  3. Keep a copy of what you sent and any response.

What to expect

A responsive landlord will acknowledge your message within a day and send someone to fix the problem. If you don't hear back within 24 hours, or if the problem continues, go to Step 3.

Step 3 5–15 min

File a 311 complaint.

This is the step that triggers the city to send an inspector. A 311 complaint creates an official record, gets HPD involved, and can result in a legal violation issued against your landlord.

How to do it — pick what's easiest
  • By phone: Call 311. If you're deaf or hard of hearing, use TTY at (212) 504-4115
  • Online: Go to portal.311.nyc.gov and search for "heat" or "hot water."
  • Mobile app: Download the NYC 311 app (iOS or Android) and file from there.
What to say on the phone
Say it in your own words

"I'd like to file a complaint about no heat (or hot water) at my apartment. The problem started on [date]. I've recorded indoor temperatures of [temperature]. I already contacted my landlord on [date] and the problem hasn't been fixed."

If anyone in your apartment is elderly, disabled, or a young child, mention it: "There is a [vulnerable person] living in the apartment."

This is a guide, not a script — use your own voice and answer their questions as they come.
What to expect
  • HPD may call your landlord first and give them a chance to fix the problem.
  • If the problem isn't fixed, HPD sends an inspector. For winter heat complaints, inspectors often arrive within 24–48 hours.
  • The inspector may arrive unannounced. If you won't be home, ask a neighbor to let them in or leave a note with a phone number.
  • If the inspector confirms the problem, they'll issue a violation against the landlord. This carries civil penalties of $250–$500 per day for a first violation, and $500–$1,000 per day for repeat violations.
Draft — pending immigration advocacy review For undocumented tenants

Your immigration status does not affect your right to safe housing.

NYC law is clear: your landlord must provide heat and hot water regardless of your immigration status, and HPD's job is to enforce that. NYC agencies including HPD and 311 do not ask about immigration status when you file a housing complaint.

It is also illegal under NYC Human Rights Law for your landlord to threaten to report you to ICE, deport you, or treat you differently because of your immigration status. If your landlord threatens you in this way, that is itself a crime you can report.

An honest note: NYC's protections are strong, but they are city and state protections. Federal immigration enforcement is a separate system. If you are worried about risks specific to your situation, you can talk to a free, confidential immigration legal organization before filing any complaint. You are not alone in asking these questions.

Worried about retaliation?

This is a real fear — here's what you should know.

Retaliation is illegal in New York. Under NY Real Property Law § 223-b, your landlord cannot punish you for making a good-faith complaint about housing conditions. Punishment includes raising your rent, refusing to renew your lease, starting an eviction, reducing services, or harassing you.

The law specifically protects complaints like this one. Filing a 311 complaint about heat or hot water is exactly the kind of action the anti-retaliation law covers.

If you believe your landlord is retaliating: document everything (dates, what happened, any written communication), and call the Met Council on Housing hotline at 212-979-0611. Retaliation cases can be fought in Housing Court, and the law is on your side.

Step 4 5 min

Check the status and keep a paper trail.

You need to know whether HPD has actually inspected, whether a violation was issued, and what the landlord has done in response. This is also the moment to decide whether to escalate.

Your complaint reference number Not entered yet — you can save it in Step 3 above.
How to do it
  1. Go to HPDOnline and enter your building's address.
  2. Look for open violations, especially under "heat" or "hot water." If your complaint resulted in a violation, you'll see it here.
  3. Note the violation number, date issued, and classification. Heat and hot water violations are typically Class C — immediately hazardous.
  4. Save a screenshot or print the page.
What to expect

Violations typically appear online within a few days of the inspection. If your complaint doesn't show up at all, the inspection may not have happened yet, or the inspector didn't find a violation — in which case, call 311 again and request another inspection.

Step 5 Varies

If nothing's working: escalate.

By this point, you've given your landlord multiple chances and the city has been involved. If you're still without heat or hot water, you have stronger options — but most involve legal or advocacy help, and you shouldn't try them alone.

A. Call the Met Council on Housing Tenants' Rights Hotline

Free, staffed by trained volunteers, and the best starting point for figuring out what to do next. They can help you decide between the options below and point you to the right legal help.

  • Phone: 212-979-0611
  • Hours: Mon & Wed 1:30–8 PM, Fri 1:30–5 PM.
  • Heads up: The hotline is very busy — call after 4 PM if you can, and avoid Mondays. Volunteer-staffed, so it occasionally closes on short notice.
B. Start an HP Action in Housing Court

A legal proceeding asking a judge to order your landlord to make repairs. You can file one yourself or with help from a free legal services organization. An HP Action can result in a court order forcing the landlord to fix the problem, plus fines.

C. If you're rent-stabilized or rent-controlled: file for a rent reduction

NYS Homes and Community Renewal (DHCR) can order a rent reduction when essential services aren't provided. You'll need the HPD inspection report from Step 4. Use Form HHW-1 or call DHCR at 833-499-0343.

D. If you qualify: free legal representation

NYC's Right to Counsel program provides free lawyers to low-income tenants facing housing issues. The Met Council hotline (option A) can help you figure out if you qualify.

Important — about rent strikes

You may have heard you can stop paying rent. This is risky.

It's technically legal to withhold rent, but if you do it without following the right legal process, your landlord can start eviction proceedings against you and you could lose your home. Met Council on Housing's advice: always consult a lawyer before withholding rent.

If you decide you need to withhold rent as a last resort, put the rent money aside every month in a separate account. Do not spend it. You may need to pay it later, and having it saved protects you if things go wrong.
